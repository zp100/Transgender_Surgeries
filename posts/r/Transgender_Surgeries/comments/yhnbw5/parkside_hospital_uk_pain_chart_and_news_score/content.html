It seems that a lot of people (including some nurses) don't know what the pain and NEWS score system in hospital relates to. So here is the page with the info. This was from Parkside Hospital UK in 2021 when I had SRS with James Bellringer.

You can see from previous posts how much pain I suffered and the mental health issues that I now have. My main issues have been caused by him rushing the operation in 75 minutes and not allowing effective pain relief. I have been left with seemingly permanent neuropathic pain from nerve damage, PTSD from the way he treated me the morning after the operation and being unable to eat or stop crying for the week in hospital due to the pain.

Pain Score

0 none no pain at rest or on movement

1 mild no pain at rest, Mild pain on movement

2 moderate Intermittent pain at rest. Moderate pain on movement

3 Continuous pain at rest, severe pain on movement.

The lied on my forms when when I was crying my eyes out in pain and scared to move.

You need to be stern with them, if they don't do anything escalate it to the ward manager then up the chain until it's sorted. Don't take no for an answer or you may end up with PTSD, still be in pain 11 months later and wishing you never woke up !

Oh my gosh, that sounds horrendous! I understand why you don't want to go under again.

It makes sense that she doesn't want you to watch!

Being prepared and taking in your own meds is a good idea; however (you may or may not know this) you're not allowed to administer your own pain meds while in hospital. So you'll either have to keep this a secret and be very discrete (which is risky because if you're out of it you could end up taking too much or have gaps which are too long) or you just be up front and show them the prescription labels (only good if they say to take every x hours or y times per day, not very helpful if they say "as required") and allow them to give you the meds every x hours. If you're feeling crappy then it can actually be really helpful if they're keeping track of time for you so that you don't get behind on the meds.

I know that you can manage your pain and meds well and better than they can. Unfortunately they do have very strict rules in place about this sort of thing (mainly to protect patients as most wouldn't be able to manage it well).

The best thing is to get the doctor to put regular pain relief on your chart (i.e. every 4 hours). You can even discuss it before the operation. Once it's on your chart then the nurses have to do it. Most of the problems come about when the pain meds are "as required" or the regular pain relief isn't sufficient. As many nurses are overworked, disillusioned and ignore suffering, getting them to give you meds "as required" is difficult and they cannot give you pain meds that a doctor hasn't authorised.

It sounds like you know what you're doing and you'll be well prepared! I really hope it goes well!
